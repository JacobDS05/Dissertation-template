\chapter*{Abstract}

Camera trap imagery is a widely used method for monitoring wildlife in their natural habitats, but the large volumes of data produced far exceed what can be manually reviewed. Meta AI's Segment Anything Model~3 (SAM~3) is a foundation model for Promptable Concept Segmentation (PCS), offering a bridge to reduce the collection-analysis gap in this domain, but the model's suitability for great ape detection in camera trap imagery remains under explored. We characterise SAM~3's performance using videos collected by the Pan African program, comparing default weights against the camera-trap-specific SA-FARI weights, and conduct a prompt engineering investigation into whether different prompt types can improve species-level discrimination. We then design two downstream classifiers, trained on SAM 3's segmentation outputs, and propose their usage as detachable model heads. We reveal that SAM~3 is an effective great ape detector, capable of achieving recall above 0.90, but cannot be relied upon to distinguish between chimpanzees and gorillas through prompting alone; the model's Perception Encoder places semantically related prompts in close regions of a shared embedding space, a limitation that prompt wording does not overcome. We present multiple downstream classifiers trained on SAM~3's segmentation outputs, each with their own strengths: a two-way classifier improving precision slightly while maintaining SAM~3's high recall, and a three-way classifier greatly improving precision at the cost of recall. The pipeline made available by such classifiers offers a practical approach to fine-grained discrimination in PCS-based wildlife analysis, an approach applicable to other domains.

\chapter{Introduction}
\label{chap:intro}

\section{Context}
\label{sec:intro_context}

\subsection{Camera Trap Imagery and The Collection-Analysis Gap}
\label{sec:intro_camtrap}

Camera trap imagery is a widely used method for observing wildlife populations in their natural habitat. These cameras can be deployed and left to record thousands of videos, reducing the need for conventional direct human observation methods to collect data on animal presence and behaviours~\cite{Tuia_2022}. The Pan~African program are using this technique across 18 field sites in Africa to capture videos of great apes, particularly chimpanzees and gorillas~\cite{brookes2024panaf20k}.

The large amount of data generated by such projects unfortunately far exceeds what could be manually reviewed by humans; one camera alone can generate thousands of videos, some of which contain no animals at all~\cite{beery2019efficientpipelinecameratrap}. Evidently, there is a gap between data collection and analysis, which must be bridged by automated analysis methods.


\subsection{The Automation Bridge}
\label{sec:intro_auto}

MegaDetector is a tool designed to filter out empty frames and localise objects (animals, people etc.) in videos~\cite{beery2019efficientpipelinecameratrap}, but is not capable of identifying the animals down to the species level. SAM~3 introduces promptable segmentation with short phrases, opening up new possibilities for this domain.

Despite these advantages, the task of robust and reliable localisation of great apes in wild camera trap footage presents several challenges. For one, the apes can appear in conventionally difficult computer vision environments, such as low quality video, variable lighting, occluded environments, facing away from the lens or at a distance from the camera \cite{brookes2024panaf20k, yang2019greatapedetectionchallenging}. Furthermore, chimpanzees and gorillas share many visually similar features, being large-bodied species with dark fur. In some of the challenging environments mentioned, it can be difficult for even a human to differentiate between the two species.

\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:intro_contributions}

The first of the project's contributions are a characterisation of SAM~3's performance, comparing the outputs produced using the default and SA-FARI weights. We find that SAM~3 proves to be an effective locator of apes generally speaking, with SAM~3 weights achieving recall of 0.90 or greater, and SA-FARI achieving higher precision at the cost of recall. Despite this, SAM~3 is not an effective species discriminator, with precision sharply decreasing when tasked with specific prompts such as ``chimpanzee" and ``gorilla".

Second, we provide a validation that careful prompt engineering alone is not sufficient to alleviate this issue. Testing a variety of prompt types, including those featuring negatives or behavioural information, resulted in a pattern where the ratio between precisions corresponds with the ratio between target sample counts. This heavily suggests that SAM~3's Perception Encoder (PE) associates the prompts too closely within a shared semantic space. No rewording attempts were enough to pull the species apart in this space.

Since the prompt engineering approach proved ineffective, we moved to designing downstream classifiers. A two-way classifier increasing end-to-end precision slightly while maintaining recall, and a three-way classifier greatly improving precision, at the cost of recall.

Hence, we propose a pipeline in which detachable model heads are an effective method to improve SAM~3 when concerned with fine-grained segmentation between similar objects. The balance between precision and recall desired would motivate the type of downstream classification one would use.



\section{Aims and Objectives}
\label{sec:aims}


This work takes an exploratory approach to investigating these problems and questions. Rather than working around a fixed hypothesis, we work through a series of investigations, with each one motivated by the last. First, we evaluate SAM~3's baseline performance with a variety of configurations, including basic prompts and alternate model weights. Then, we investigate whether a prompt engineering approach can be used to yield more desired outputs, as this is an under explored area. Finally, after establishing the limits posed by this approach, we design purpose built classification models using ResNet~\cite{he2015deepresiduallearningimage}, proposing the practicality of using such models in combination with SAM~3 to alleviate some of the shortfalls of using SAM~3 alone.

The project aims to improve the robustness of localisation of great apes in wild camera trap imagery, but more concretely, the aims and objectives are as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Develop an initial understanding of SAM~3's performance, including common success and failure cases, in localising, tracking and differentiating between great apes.
    \item Investigate the impact of prompt engineering in improving SAM~3 detection accuracy and species-level discrimination.
    \item Design and compare species classification methods, leveraging SAM~3 outputs as training data.
    \item Propose a segmentation-classification pipeline, integrating SAM~3 with the best-performing classifier to improve species-level discrimination.
\end{enumerate}

\section{The PanAf20K Dataset}

    \caption[PanAf20K Annotations]{Examples of annotations for PanAf20K. PanAf500 annotations (left) feature tight full-body bounding boxes, and frame-level behavioural actions assigned to each ape. PanAf20K annotations (right) provide only video-level behavioural annotations, without bounding boxes or ape assignment. \textit{Reproduced from \cite{brookes2024panaf20k}}}
    \label{fig:annotations}
\end{figure}

PanAf20K, collected by the Pan 
African Program, is a set of 20,000 static camera trap videos (roughly 7 million frames) featuring chimpanzees and gorillas in their natural habitats, \cite{brookes2024panaf20k, brookes2024panaf20kdataset} taken across 18 field sites in tropical Africa (Figure~\ref{fig:panaf}). PanAf20K contains a fully annotated subset of 500 videos (over 180K frames), referred to as PanAf500. These fine-grained annotations feature species classification, behavioural actions, bounding boxes and associated intra-video tracking IDs. The remainder of PanAf20K contains multi-label annotations indicating behaviours present in the video without associated apes or spacial locations. See Figure~\ref{fig:annotations} for details concerning each annotation type.

Because a subset of PanAf500 was used to train the SA-FARI model weights, the remaining 71 videos, which we shall refer to as PanAf71, were used for the initial evaluation of SAM~3 performance in Chapter~\ref{chap:execution}.

\section{The SA-FARI Dataset}

SA-FARI is the largest open-source Multi-Animal Tracking (MAT) dataset for wild animals \cite{wasmuht2025safaridatasetsegmentfootage}. It contains over 11,000 camera trap videos, each with manually verified, spatio-temporal segmentation annotations. This dataset was used to fine-tune SAM~3 weights; using SA-FARI weights aims to adapt the general purpose model to our camera-trap specific domain. The fine-tuned model outperforms SAM~3 on SA-FARI's own benchmarks~\cite{wasmuht2025safaridatasetsegmentfootage}, and shows improved precision at the cost of recall when used as an alternate configuration for SAM~3 in this work.

A subset of PanAf500 was used to train the fine-tuned model checkpoint, motivating our usage of PanAf71, the videos which were not used in model training, for SAM~3 evaluation. In Section~\ref{sec:execution_classification2}, we ensure the test and validation dataset splits only contain PanAf71 videos to avoid data leakage.


\section{Existing Camera Trap Analysis Approaches}
\label{sec:bg_existing}

In camera trap analysis, there has historically been a separation between detection and classification. In this section, we compare and contrasts works relating to both stages, starting with a generic animal detector, then looking at state-of-the-art works which are more adapted to the great ape domain. Through this, we motivate this work's proposed pipeline.

\subsection{MegaDetector}

MegaDetector is an open source detector trained on millions of images featuring humans, vehicles and animals, meaning it is commonly used for camera trap analysis. It works by discarding empty frames in videos, and providing bounding boxes corresponding to objects in the video. Fennell et al.~\cite{Fennell2022.01.14.476404} report a recall of 92\% on animals at a confidence threshold of 90\%.

Though this is a great tool for automated detection, it comes with its own disadvantages. For one, the model is not able to classify animals, calling for further manual or automated methods when classification is necessary. Second, bounding boxes do not provide the exact spatial locations, but give a boundary in which the object is contained. Thus, despite excellent detection performance, users of MegaDetector must conduct further review~\cite{velez2022choosingappropriateplatformworkflow}.

\subsection{Deep Learning Approaches for Species Classification}

A common field in camera trap analysis is species classification, with many works utilising CNNs and transfer learning. Norouzzadeh et al.~\cite{norouzzadeh2017automaticallyidentifyingcountingdescribing} trained a range of models, including ResNet, on 3.2 million images featuring 48 species. ResNet achieved a top-1 accuracy of 93.8\% in identifying species in images, establishing the viability of CNNs as species classifiers. Further research shows that fine-tuning existing models, even if the base does not coincide with the target dataset, often outperforms CNNs built from scratch~\cite{willi2019identifying}.

Despite high accuracy, such deep-learning based classifiers have disadvantages. First, they require large, balanced datasets for training, often requiring multi-institutional effort to collate data. Additionally, these classifiers are often trained on entire images containing species, which can cause the models to infer information from the background rather than the intended object. This limitation motivates this work's exploration into using pixel-accurate masks as training data.


\subsection{Great Ape Detection and Recognition}

The approaches above apply to any wildlife, great ape detection specifically has received special attention due to conservation concerns. A large amount of work for analysing camera trap imagery featuring great apes specifically has been and will continue to be led by the University of Bristol.

Yang et al.~\cite{yang2019greatapedetectionchallenging} designed a great ape detector, utilising temporal information and full-body morphology to improve detections in challenging environments when compared with frame based detectors. They achieve a 91.17\% mAP on PanAf500, outperforming frame-based state-of-the-art detectors. This highlights the impact of utilising the temporal domain.

Schofield et al.~\cite{schofield2019chimpanzee} proposed a fully automated pipeline using CNNs to detect, track and recognise chimpanzees by their faces. Where Yang et al. use full-body morphology to detect apes, this pipeline utilised facial information to identify apes and their sex. They achieved 92.5\% accurate identity recognition and 96.2\% accuracy for sex recognition. Of course, there is a limitation in that faces must be visible. Brust et al.~\cite{brust2017gorilla} conducted similar research combining detection and classification models for gorillas, resulting in a 90.8\% AP and individual identification accuracy of 80.3\%.

Each of this studies encapsulate a similar workflow seen in the classification section of this work. Importantly, no prior work has applied a PCS model like SAM~3. Contrastingly, this work will replace traditional detection methods using SAM~3, injecting a modern perspective into a traditional pipeline.

\subsection{Summary}

To summarise, we show that widely used models like MegaDetector can efficiently detect animals in camera trap imagery, bridging the collection-analysis gap somewhat. Additionally, CNNs prove effective models for species classification and great ape identity. Finally, we see that modern approaches suggest that temporal information can be used to improve such models. The approach taken in this work will combine many of these conventional methods, using SAM~3 masks as classifier training data to improve upon the model's failures in this domain.




\section{Segment Anything Model (SAM) History}

Meta AI's SAM~3 is central to this project, but to understand its design and some of the choices motivating the project, we must understand its history.

\subsection{SAM}

The first Segment Anything Model~\cite{kirillov2023segment} allowed users to provide an image and a `prompt', be it a point in an image or a bounding box, and would generate a pixel accurate mask for all corresponding objects in an image. It was trained using a vast dataset of 1 billion masks across 11 million images called SA-1B, giving it strong generalisation to objects it had never encountered.

The model was limited to single images, and could not yet accept text phrases as prompts, though this functionality was being considered at the time of development.

\subsection{SAM 2}

SAM~2~\cite{ravi2024sam2segmentimages} extends the original model by enabling segmentation across videos. The transformer architecture allows users to request segmentation of an object as they would with SAM, and will generate segmentation mask sequences, known as `masklets', keeping object identity consistent in the temporal domain. Not only does the model build on SAM's functionality, but proves to be a more accurate option, at $6\times$ the speed. The SA-V dataset used to train this model contains 642.6K masklets between 50.9K videos.

\subsection{SAM 3}

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{images/sam3.png}
    \caption[SAM 3 Usage Examples]{Examples highlighting SAM~3's usage of PCS, allowing users to segment all instances of an object specified by a short text prompt. \textit{Reproduced from \cite{carion2025sam3segmentconcepts}.}}
    \label{fig:sam3}
\end{figure}

SAM~3~\cite{carion2025sam3segmentconcepts} introduces text-based prompting, newly referred to as Promptable Concept Segmentation (PCS) by the author, demonstrated in Figure~\ref{fig:sam3}. Given a prompt (e.g. ``chimpanzee") the model will localise and track objects between frames. This functions similarly to SAM~2, but instead of the user inputting where the object is, they indicate what they are looking for. The training dataset for SAM~3 known as SA-CO is made up of over 3M media-phrase pairs, built by 207K unique phrases and 121K images and videos.

This functionality is the basis for Section~\ref{sec:execution_prompt}, since prompting a vision model is a new and under explored field.


\section{Prompting Vision-Language Models (VLMs)}

Manipulating natural language prompts is a relatively new area of research, particularly for vision-language models. Radford et al.~\cite{radford2021learningtransferablevisualmodels} introduced zero-shot classification by comparing image embeddings to embeddings of natural-language class descriptions. They found that prompts starting with ``a photo of a" rather than just being a single word achieved better performance at an increase of 1.3\% on ImageNet, due to the model being trained on full sentences and not single words. This establishes the method of reformulating prompts, similar to the investigation conducted in this work.

Zhou et al.~\cite{zhou2022conditionalpromptlearningvisionlanguage} build on this by creating a model (CoOp) which can turn manually designed prompts into reformulated prompts which achieve greater performance on CLIP than intensively-tuned manual prompts. They expand on this by building CoCoOp, which improves generalisation, alleviating the main weakness of CoOp.

Despite the above works regarding prompt engineering for VLMs, prompting SAM~3 for segmentation specifically remains an under explored field.




\section{Evaluation Metrics}

% To characterise spatial accuracy, the F1-score and AP (Average Precision) \cite{cvalgosmetrics} were adopted. To quantify tracking performance, MOTA (Multiple Object Tracking Accuracy) and IDF1 (Identification F1-score) \cite{MOTmetrics} were adopted.

This section defines the metrics used to evaluate SAM~3 performance in Chapter~\ref{chap:execution}.

\subsection{Intersection over Union}

In this project, SAM~3 outputs are considered correct, if they overlap with the ground truth data with an Intersection over Union (IoU) above a threshold of 0.5. Thus, the metrics in this section depend on the IoU. Formally, we define IoU as:
\begin{equation}
    IoU = \frac{|B_p \cap B_{gt}|}{|B_p \cup B_{gt}|}
\end{equation}
where $B_p$ and $B_{gt}$ denote the predicted and ground truth bounding box regions respectively.


\subsection{Precision, Recall, F1}

Precision measures the proportion of correct detections, and recall measures the proportion of ground truth objects which are detected:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}
    \label{eq:precision}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \text{Recall} = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}
    \label{eq:recall}
\end{equation}
where $TP$ denotes true positives, $FP$ denotes false positives and $FN$ denotes false negatives (Table~\ref{tab:outcomecmatrix}.
These two metrics are central to the findings which motivated the research conducted in this work.

F1 is the harmonic mean between precision and recall, essentially providing a quantitative measure of how `good' a model is based off the precision-recall balance. It is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    F1 = \frac{2 \cdot \text{Precision} \cdot \text{Recall}}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}
\end{equation}

\subsection{(Mean) Average Precision}

Average Precision (AP) is defined as the area under the precision-recall curve, computed by sweeping the confidence thresholds of each detection.
Mean Average Precision (mAP) is computed by taking the average AP across all classes. In the context of this project $AP \equiv mAP$, because we only ever calculate across one class.

\subsection{Tracking Metrics}

We adopt MOTA and IDF1 to quantify tracking performance~\cite{MOTmetrics}.

MOTA evaluates overall tracking accuracy by accounting for false positives, false negatives and the number of identity switches between frames. It is computed by:
\begin{equation}
    MOTA = 1 - \frac{FN + FP + IDSW}{GT}
\end{equation}
where $IDSW$ denotes the number of identity switches and $GT$ denotes the number of ground truth objects. Note, that even if $IDSW = 0$, this MOTA can be negative if $FN + FP > GT$, so a low MOTA does not necessarily indicate that temporal consistency alone is underperforming.

Rather than penalising all identity switches between each frames equally, IDF1 considers how well the predicted track path compares with the ground truth, rewarding long unbroken tracking. Therefore, it is more sensitive to whether a model maintains consistent identity. Formally:
\begin{equation}
    IDF1 = 
    \frac{2 \cdot IDTP}
    {2 \cdot IDTP + IDFP + IDFN}
\end{equation}
where $IDTP$, $IDFP$ and $IDFN$ represent identity true positives, false positives and false negatives respectively.



\section{Deep Learning for Classification}

This section provides an explanation of the techniques used in Section~\ref{sec:execution_classification2}.

\subsection{Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN)}

CNNs are a version of neural networks designed specifically for structured, grid-like data~\cite{Goodfellow-et-al-2016}, perfect for the use of images in this project. Their layered architecture allows early layers to learn low-level features, such as edges and textures, where late layers learn high-level features, such as general shape of an object in an image. This is achieved by each block analysing a small section of an image using convolution to identify important features.


\subsection{Residual Networks}
\label{sec:bg_resnet}

Inspired by VGG nets \cite{simonyan2015deepconvolutionalnetworkslargescale}, ResNet is built on top of a plain network, being an initial convolutional layer and a further four residual building blocks \cite{he2015deepresiduallearningimage}. What turns this plain network into its residual counterpart, is the insertion of shortcut connections which aims to mitigate the degradation problem seen in deeper networks. The formal definition of a building block is:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:resnet}
    \textbf{y} = \mathcal{F}(\textbf{x}, \{W_i\}) + \textbf{x}
\end{equation}
where $\textbf{x}$ and $\textbf{y}$ are the input and output of the block respectively, $\mathcal{F}$ denotes the residual mapping, and $\{W_i\}$ is the set of learnable parameters associated with $\mathcal{F}$. The function $\mathcal{F}$ represents the sequence of convolutional layers and non-linear functions such as ReLU.

This contrasts a simple feed-forward CNN, where each layer takes the output of the previous layer. Residual Networks instead learn the residual function then add the unchanged input.

\subsection{Fine-Tuning}

Fine-tuning is a process where you take an existing model and its weights initialised via training on some dataset (in our case, ResNet trained on ImageNet) and adapt the model to a more specific domain. Here we adapt ResNet-18 by unfreezing the model weights and training them on an ape dataset to build an effective two-way classifier.

\subsection{Loss Function and Optimization}

Each classifier is trained with cross-entropy loss. For binary classification, this is formally defined as:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} =  -\frac{1}{N} \sum^N_{i=1}{\left[ y_i \log(p_i) + (1 - y_i) \log(1 - p_i) \right]}
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the number of samples, $y_i \in \{0, 1\}$ is the true label and $p_i \in \left[0, 1 \right]$ is the predicted probability of observation $i$ being in class 1. Minimising $\mathcal{L}$ is an effective way to improve accuracy during training, ensuring the model effectively distinguishes between two classes.

For optimisation, we adopt Adam, an algorithm effective for use on large datasets~\cite{kingma2017adammethodstochasticoptimization}.

\section{Two-Way Species Classification}
\label{sec:execution_classification2}

It is clear that SAM~3 does not effectively distinguish the two ape species in the dataset. We have also shown that prompt design is not an effective method in alleviating this issue. Thus, an alternative approach must be taken in order to improve species-level classification.

This section discusses two classification models: a simple CNN built from scratch and a fine-tuned ResNet model. Each model was trained using the same data.

\subsection{Dataset}

\subsubsection{Image Preparation}

Due to SAM~3's ability to detect the majority of apes in each video, it was possible to utilise its outputs to train each model. The training setup is as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Prepare the PanAf500 videos for processing with SAM~3.
    \item Generate masklets with SAM~3's highest recall configuration (shown in Table~\ref{tab:metricsinitial}): default weights, and prompt ``ape".
    \item Verify outputs against ground truth annotations, keeping correct predictions.
    \item Crop each frame down to the corresponding bounding boxes, zeroing out the background using the predicted masks.
    \item Save each cropped region as an image, assigning an appropriate label (\texttt{chimpanzee} or \texttt{gorilla}). We will refer to these images as crops.
    \item Split into training, testing, and validation data. The validation and testing splits were made up of PanAf71 videos to avoid data leakage.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Data Augmentation}

For each classification model, the following transforms were applied to the training split before training the model:
\begin{itemize}
    \item All images were resized to a resolution of $224 \times 224$ pixels.
    \item Images were horizontally flipped with a probability of $0.5$
    \item All images were rotated by a randomly selected angle between $-20^\circ$ and $20^\circ$. The small angle range ensures objects are not rotated to appear at some orientation by which they would not likely appear in any raw camera trap imagery.
\end{itemize}
Augmenting the data in such a way aims to improve the model's generalisation, consequently increasing their accuracy on unseen data.



\subsection{Training Process}
\label{sec:execution_training}

Both models were trained using the same process for the sake of comparison.

\subsubsection{Setup}

A cross entropy loss function was employed due to its effectiveness in binary classifiers \cite{crossentropy}. We adopt Adam optimisation \cite{kingma2017adammethodstochasticoptimization} with a learning rate of $1e-3$ and weight decay of $1e-5$, balancing how quickly the model learns and loss penalty size to prevent overfitting.

Additionally, we define an early stopping condition to further prevent overfitting: take the model achieving the lowest validation loss after every epoch, terminating training if there has been no improvement for a set patience level (10 epochs).

Finally, we adopt a `frame skip' parameter: this controls how many frames are skipped when preparing the dataset, aiming to reduce the data overfitting as a result of a large number of visually similar frames. For each model, the frame skip interval was set to 5.

\subsubsection{Loop}

The training loop can be described as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Prepare crops and labels
    \item Complete a forward pass through the network
    \item Compute the loss and backpropagate, updating parameters using gradient descent
    \item Measure performance on the train and validation splits using loss and classification accuracy. Accuracy was computed by taking the ratio of correct versus total predictions.
    \item Repeat until a fixed number of epochs (200) has been completed, or when the early stopping condition is met.
\end{enumerate}

It is important to note that the baseline accuracy is roughly equivalent to the percentage of crops associated with the chimpanzee label. This could indicate that the model is biased towards choosing the majority class in the dataset. Thus, it is unlikely that the model is effective at differentiating between the two classes, rendering the accuracy misleading.

The additional increase of roughly 18\% in accuracy demonstrates the effectiveness of fine tuning pre-existing models. Though the baseline is trained on a diverse dataset with many class labels, it is not optimised to effectively predict chimpanzee versus gorilla based on their visual features. Fine-tuning alleviates this issue, allowing us to purpose build the model to our use case.


In Figure~\ref{fig:resnetcmatrix} we see a clear difference in the number of predictions for each class when compared with Figure~\ref{fig:apenetcmatrix}. The model output \texttt{chimpanzee} 3,586 times and \texttt{gorilla} 2,149 times. This indicates that, unlike ApeNet, the model is not just outputting the majority class for almost every crop, but has learned features of each target class, and is leveraging this information when making a decision. The vast improvement can likely be attributed to the model's architecture, with more blocks / layers allowing the model to effectively learn the low and high level features.

In Figure~\ref{fig:resnetcurves}, we see that training loss decreases smoothly, from 0.32 to 0.06 over the 19 epoch window, indicating strong optimisation. On the other hand, validation loss oscillates rapidly, often with a jump as high as 0.98 between epoch 14 and 15. This pattern heavily indicates overfitting, likely due to the training data. The camera trap videos feature many similar consecutive frames, and the size of the validation set constitutes a small percentage of PanAf500 (3\%). The early stopping condition (\S~\ref{sec:execution_training}) selected the model with the lowest validation loss after 10 epochs, being the epoch 9 checkpoint.

Figure~\ref{fig:resnet2failures} highlights some of the misclassifications produced by the fine-tuned ResNet. The relative sizes of each crop has been retained, to demonstrate that the frequency of small crops is large; the model struggles to identify the species given a low quality image of an ape far from the camera. Some of the apes are cut-off by the camera's field of view, meaning the classifier can only interpret half of the full ape. Some appear as though they do not represent the full body of an ape (bottom right), likely due to inaccurate masks or an overly generous IoU threshold when building the crop dataset. Finally, many of the images feature heavy occlusion, meaning the mask does not take the shape of the ape, but is also characterised by the vegetation obscuring it.

\chapter{Critical Evaluation}
\label{chap:evaluation}

In this chapter, we interpret the findings for each of the three sections in Chapter~\ref{chap:execution}, and complete a critical evaluation of the methods and limitations. We then discuss to what extent the aims and objectives (Section~\ref{sec:aims}) have been achieved.

\section{SAM 3 Detection Performance Evaluation}

\subsection{Analysis}

One of the most consistent patterns, revealed in Section~\ref{sec:execution_initial}, is the difference in the precision-recall trade-off when switching between alternate weight configurations. The default SAM~3 weights achieve a consistently high recall ($\ge0.90$), but with a maximum precision of 0.53. On the other hand, the SA-FARI weights often yield marginally lower recall than SAM~3, in favour of higher precision, at a maximum of 0.60. This behaviour is expected, due to the design and intended purpose of each of the weights. Where SAM~3 is designed as a general purpose model for segmenting anything~\cite{carion2025sam3segmentconcepts}, SA-FARI was built to fine-tune the model for the purpose of camera trap analysis~\cite{wasmuht2025safaridatasetsegmentfootage}. SAM~3's over detection is likely a result of two factors: first, the model is designed preferring over detection to under detection. Secondly, the model is of course not designed to detect great apes in wild camera trap imagery, so the challenging conditions like dark areas of foliage sharing visual similarity to ape features, are limiting the number of correct detections. SA-FARI is designed to tackle these failures, and is thus biased to making more conservative detections.

The most notable finding is the drastic precision loss concerning species-specific detections. While the high recall is maintained, the prompts ``chimpanzee" and ``gorilla" result in a SAM~3 precision of 0.29 and 0.17 respectively, with SA-FARI achieving 0.38 and 0.18. This is a clear indicator that the model is producing roughly the same set of detections for each prompt, and the higher precision achieved by chimpanzees being caused by the greater number of chimpanzee samples in the dataset. This claim is further supported in Section~\ref{sec:execution_prompt}, where we reveal that the ratio between precision scores in behavioural prompting is roughly equal to the ratio between sample counts. We can deduce that the model is not differentiating between the species in each prompt, but responding to some broader contextual features.

To validate this, we look to the SAM~3's text encoder \cite{carion2025sam3segmentconcepts}. The Perception Encoder (PE)~\cite{bolya2025perceptionencoderbestvisual} allows SAM~3 to learn about concepts using a shared semantic space, not by using an exact lookup table. That is, the model can derive meaning from related concepts. It is likely that ``chimpanzee" and ``gorilla" exist in a close semantic space, where their broader great ape features have much more of an impact in the space than their species-specific traits. This is not necessarily a flaw of SAM~3's design, but a limitation of such large-scale vision-language models.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:evalsam3lim}

One limitation of this experiment, is that PanAf71, the subset used to evaluate the model, is small and the precision figures are heavily influenced by each species' sample size. If a larger subset with a more even split of objects was used, we would likely see the precision differences between each species-specific test narrow. Should we have had access to more data with fine-grained annotations, or the time to annotate some PanAf20K videos, a larger subset with a more even split would have been desirable.

Another, is that we use a conventional IoU threshold of 0.5. At this level, it is likely that some of the false positives were correctly detecting apes, but the localisation accuracy was too poor to be verified as correct. Raising or lowering the IoU would exclude more detections or accept new ones. This IoU is sufficient to provide an idea of SAM~3's strengths and weaknesses when used consistently throughout the project, but some other threshold levels could have been investigated given more time.

Finally, alternate SAM~3 minimum confidence levels could have been investigated. The low threshold of 0.1 could be causing the disparity between precision and recall, and higher thresholds could have provided a more balanced harmony.




\section{Prompt Engineering Evaluation}

\subsection{Analysis}

Section~\ref{sec:execution_prompt} demonstrated that prompt engineering, despite affecting model outputs, is not a viable method to improve species-accurate detections.

Section~\ref{sec:prompt_behaviour} clearly highlights this, by the observation that the ratio between precisions ($1.82$) and the ratio between sample counts ($1.95$) are roughly equal, with approximately equal recall scores for both behavioural prompts (``ape sitting": 0.93, ``ape walking": 0.91). This indicates that SAM~3 is not responding to the behavioural component of the prompt, because if it was, each of the precisions would be higher and the ratio between the two would not correspond with the dataset composition.

The taxonomy findings in Section~\ref{subsec:prompt_taxonomy} solidify this argument. We expected the broader taxonomic rankings to yield greater or similar recall, and divergent rankings to improve precision, neither of which was the case. Performance was roughly equal across all 5 broader taxonomic rankings ($F1$ between $0.70$ and $0.73$), again plummeting species-specific prompts were evaluated, with an F1-score of 0.42 and 0.43 for ``pan troglodytes" and ``gorilla gorilla" respectively. Evidently, taxonomy cannot be used to manipulate the output in any way that would warrant considering taxonomy-based prompts over common English phrases.

Another finding is that SAM~3 is somewhat sensitive prompt formulations. Though semantically equivalent phrases like ``chimpanzee and gorilla" and ``gorilla and chimpanzee" did achieve slightly different results, the difference is negligible, with these prompts having an F1-score of 0.69 and 0.71 respectively with SA-FARI weights. This difference is negligible however, with no way to reformulate prompts in such a way as to significantly improve performance, as seen in Section~\ref{subsec:prompt_formulations}. This solidifies that the model is not reasoning about prompts, but associates them with a general semantic space.

Finally, Section~\ref{subsec:prompt_formulations} also considers whether prompts containing negatives could be used to improve precision for species-level localisation tasks. For example, the prompt ``chimpanzee" produced a precision of 0.28 on the 20-video subset, equivalent to the precision of prompt ``chimpanzee and not gorilla", again supporting the claim above.

We have shown in a variety of ways that SAM~3 associates prompts with a general semantic space, and is not performing any sort of logical reasoning or interpretation of the prompts. Hence, prompts should be treated as a general identifier, such that deciding the formulation of the prompt should not be an important factor when using the model. Therefore, by showing that there is no clear way to engineer prompting to improve great ape detection with SAM~3, we improve the robustness by designing a classification head, leveraging SAM~3's masklets as input.

\subsection{Limitations}

One limitation with this investigation is that there were other prompt designs that could have been used in the investigation. The most notable is the functionality allowing users to provide an example image as a prompt, a feature carried over from SAM~2. Though it cannot be said for certain that other phrases or image prompts would not produce better results, the volume of negative results showing similar patterns above provide a convincing argument that other methods would also produce negative results. Given more time, we would most likely choose to investigate image prompts, as the conclusions derived in this section do only apply to text-based prompts.

Another limitation, similar to that given in Section~\ref{sec:evalsam3lim}, is that the 20-video subset used to evaluate prompts is very small, even smaller than PanAf71. This means that, though we have been able to demonstrate prompt sensitivity, a larger dataset would provide a clear picture of the true variance between prompts.

Finally, an open question remains; since only SA-FARI weights were used in the prompt investigation, it is not clear whether fine-tuning for this domain impacts prompts. Perhaps the SA-FARI weights do affect the model's associations with the semantic space, but we would not be able to say unless a similar investigation was carried out with the default weights.


\section{Classification Evaluation}
\label{sec:evalclass}

\subsection{Analysis}

\subsubsection{Two-Way}

In Section~\ref{sec:execution_classification2}, we see that fine-tuning a ResNet model outperforms both the baseline model and a small custom CNN (ApeNet), with ResNet achieving a test accuracy of 85\% and ApeNet achieving 67\% (albeit, with a caveat).

Initially, ApeNet's 67\% test accuracy appears to indicate that the model has learned to discriminate between the two species somewhat and achieve decent performance, but inspecting the model's failure cases reveals that this is not the case. Out of 5,375 total predictions, just 15 of these were \texttt{gorilla}. The 67\% accuracy is therefore roughly equivalent to the percentage of chimpanzees in the test set, and the accuracy that would be achieved by a \texttt{for}~loop returning \texttt{chimpanzee} for every frame. So, ApeNet has not learned to differentiate between species, but instead has learned that it will perform fairly well by predicting the majority class nearly every time.

The fine-tuned ResNet-18 model improves on this model by around 18pp. The first reason for this is that the pretrained ImageNet provides a much stronger initialisation than ApeNet, which has no prior knowledge of any image data. Another, is that the larger architecture and residual connections allow the model to better encode the features of the two visually similar species. Given the visual similarity of the two great ape species, the 18pp increase is higher than expected. This could be down to the design decision of using masks as input to the model, rather than using entire crops featuring background information which the model could be sensitive to.

85\% is a decent score for a two-way classifier such as this, especially given the visually similar targets. Other models, such as Snapshot Serengeti~\cite{norouzzadeh2017automaticallyidentifyingcountingdescribing} have proven that greater accuracy is achievable in similar domains. Norouzzadeh et al.~\cite{norouzzadeh2017automaticallyidentifyingcountingdescribing} report a 92.1\% top-1 accuracy, but across 48 distinct species and with 3.2 million images in the training split. The task of chimpanzee versus gorilla in this project is more challenging, due to the smaller dataset size available and the similar visual features shared by each species. Thus, 85\% is decent, but could be improved in some ways which were not explored, such as further parameter tuning and an increased dataset size.

The final end-to-end metrics demonstrated that utilising this model as a downstream classifier would achieve an increased precision, sacrificing recall. Due to the two-way design, the model was forced to make incorrect decisions, having to classify SAM~3's false positives as chimpanzees or gorillas. This motivated the decision to retune ResNet-18 with false positives and a third output label: \texttt{not-ape}.

\subsubsection{Three-Way}

The three-way classifier achieved an overall accuracy of roughly 75\% on the test set. This is 10pp lower than the accuracy achieved by the two-way classifier, but this is expected due to the introduction of a third class and the heavy class imbalance.

Though the overall accuracy is lower, the end-to-end precision can be argued to have improved on the two-way classifier, depending on the use case. F1 is still greater than anything achieved by species-specific prompts with SAM~3 alone for both classes, though the chimpanzee F1 is lower than that achieved by the two-way classifier. This is due to an inverse imbalance problem compared to the lone use of SAM~3. We now see precisions of 0.70 and 0.66 for chimpanzees and gorillas respectively, but the recall is $\leq50$. Thus, should species accurate detections be required, the three-way classification model would be an ideal downstream classifier, with the two-way model providing a more balanced option.

\subsection{Limitations}

The most glaring limitation is in the design of ApeNet. The problem of the model learning to predict the majority class could have been avoided by a number of conventional methods, including using a balanced dataset, initialising weights to discourage predicting \texttt{chimpanzee} somewhat, or a class-weighted cross-entropy loss function. The current setup may make the gain in using fine-tuning over a simple CNN seem higher than it truly is.

Additionally, for both models, the size of the validation set is small. It was difficult to make the test or validation set larger due to the limitation of them having to include only PanAf71 videos to avoid data leakage. Cross validation could have been used to ensure the result is not a result of sampling; it is plausible that a different dataset split could have achieved a different test set accuracy.

% Third, rather than building a model head to be compatible with SAM~3 outputs, fine-tuning the SAM~3 model or restructuring the architecture was considered. This idea was rejected for a number of reasons. Firstly, the architecture is very large and adapting this to our task is outside the scope of the project. Secondly, adapting the model to a task as specific as this would go against what the model was designed for. Also, we have shown that SAM~3 is not highly sensitive to prompts, so it is unclear whether adapting the model for this would even be possible, as it does not appear to be attempting to differentiate between the two classes. Finally, the amount of data required for such a task far exceeds what was available when conducting this work.


\section{Alternative Approaches}
\label{sec:eval_alternatives}

Many approaches could have been taken to carry out the work. This section will discuss any options that were considered, and justify their exclusion from the project.

\subsection{Image Prompts}

SAM~3 allows input of images as prompts, providing an alternative to short text prompts. The user can provide an image featuring the desired object, and SAM~3 will segment all instances of that object in images and videos.


\subsection{Fine-Tune SAM 3}

Instead of designing a classification head to be used on SAM~3's outputs, fine-tuning the model itself seems like a sensible option, but a number of reasons motivated the decision to use a model head. For one, attaching model heads is a design choice which allows easy replacement or removal from SAM~3. Additionally this allows segmentation and classification to be reasoned about separately: if one were to fine-tune the base model, it could be that classification is improved while segmentation is worsened, for example.

Though the prompt investigation makes it seem as though SAM~3 cannot differentiate between species, it could be that the visual model does have sufficient features to make such distinctions, but the text encoder is not sufficient for prompts to access this. Fine-tuning the model with species-labelled data would alleviate this, as was the case with SA-FARI~\cite{wasmuht2025safaridatasetsegmentfootage}. The amount of samples labelled chimpanzee and gorilla in this dataset was rather small, perhaps not enough to overcome SAM~3's close association between the two species. Considering SA-FARI was a multi-institutional project, conducting similar work, would require a huge amount of data and time, far exceeding the scope of this project.




\subsection{Prompt-Based Ensemble Classifier}

Before discovering that different prompts could not be used to retrieve any useful information, an ensemble classifier combining outputs from many different prompts was conceived. The idea was a probabilistic model in which the outputs yielded by each prompt acted as a model `expert', where different prompt outputs could be combined to decide on a class label. For example, you could combine many chimpanzee-based prompts such as ``slender chimpanzee", ``chimpanzee with light face" with gorilla-based prompts like ``bulky gorilla", ``gorilla with silver back", and if the chimpanzee prompts highlighted the same object more frequently than the gorilla prompts, the model would output \texttt{chimpanzee}. Of course, the exact model design was not considered, since prompt engineering proved ineffective, motivating the idea to be discarded.

\chapter{Conclusion}
\label{chap:conclusion}

The goal of this project was to consider whether SAM~3, a recent model for prompt-based segmentation, could be adapted to robustly detect great apes in wild camera trap imagery, aiming to tackle a domain in which there is a large gap between the volume of data and the tools to automatically analyse such data. We develop a clear insight into the model's strengths and weaknesses, and propose methods to tackle the weaknesses.

We first demonstrate that, broadly, SAM~3 is an effective ape detector, but is not capable of discriminating between apes at the species-level. Using the PanAf71 set for evaluation, we achieve recall of at least 0.90 with the default weights, and show that SA-FARI weights increase precision at the cost of recall, as intended. However, when using species-specific prompts like ``chimpanzee" or ``gorilla", recall remained high and precision collapsed, with the ratio between precision scores relating to the ratio of each species' sample counts. This indicates that the model produced near identical outputs for either prompt, suggesting that the model's PE forces it to associate each prompt with in a close semantic-space.

We reinforced this conclusion in the prompt investigation, by demonstrating that prompt engineering could not be leveraged to yield more desirable outputs. Negatives did not sway the model to avoid a particular object, taxonomy based prompts did not improve recall or precision in any useful way, and behavioural prompts failed to invoke any behaviour-aware segmentation. Together, the results suggest that SAM~3 does not reason about prompts in some logical way, but treats prompts as points in a semantic space. Hence, it is reasonable that ``chimpanzee" and ``gorilla" lie close together in some semantic space, dominated by being apes, such that no wording could pull them apart.

Motivated by the negative result produced by prompt engineering, we instead look to the conventional method of designing CNNs for classification, utilising SAM~3's masks as an under explored design choice for training. ApeNet produced poor predictive performance, having learned to predict the majority class in over 99\% of its total predictions. This was mainly due to class imbalance and small-scale model architecture. The fine-tuned ResNet-18 model improved on ApeNet's 67\% accuracy by 18pp at roughly 85\%. While this figure is not perfect, it represents improvement over usage of SAM~3 alone, and proposes that implementing such model heads for similar tasks are a viable option when discrimination between multiple objects sharing a similar semantic space is a requirement.

A three-way classifier was then trained using the large volume of false positives produced by SAM~3 to add a negative label (\texttt{not-ape}). This achieved a worse overall accuracy on the test set (75\%), but greatly improved species-level precision when evaluated as a full end-to-end pipeline.

\section{Contributions}

This work contributes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A comprehensive characterisation of SAM~3's performance for camera trap analysis of great apes in the wild, including with usafe of SA-FARI weights.
    \item Solid evidence that prompt engineering cannot be used to improve performance in any meaningful way, due to the way text is encoded as points in a semantic space, causing closely related objects to be perceived similarly.
    \item A demonstration that fine-tuned model training is a strong way to improve species-level discrimination for this domain.
    \item A proposed segmentation-classification pipeline combining the SAM~3's outputs with a model head, utilising SAM~3's strong detective performance.
\end{itemize}

\section{Future Work}

There are many directions that follow from these findings. First, an investigation into SAM~3's functionality to accept images as prompts was not investigated, and could provide a way to bypass the limitations posed by the PE's semantic associations. Second, fine-tuning SAM~3 for this task would test whether poor species-discrimination is a structural limitation or due to a lack of training data. Finally, the tracking performance revealed in Section~\ref{sec:execution_initial} left much to be desired, so further investigation into improving MOTA and IDF1 is logical.

The broader takeaway is that SAM~3 is a useful tool for analysing camera trap imagery featuring great apes, so long as species specific detections are not essential. Should this be required, the model must be adapted to the specific domain, with work proposing a simple pipeline to improve precision at the species level. Though this project concerns great apes, it is useful to note that the methods discussed in this work are not unique to great apes or camera trap imagery, and can easily be adapted for any domain.

\paragraph{Precision}

Precision measures the proportion of correct predicted detections.
\begin{equation}
    \text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}
\end{equation}
where $TP$ denotes the number of true positives, and $FP$ denotes the number of false positives. High precision indicates that the model produces few incorrect detections.


\paragraph{Recall}

Recall measures the proportion of ground truth objects that are correctly detected.
\begin{equation}
    \text{Recall} = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}
\end{equation}
where $FN$ denotes the number of false negatives. High recall indicates that few ground truth objects go undetected.


\paragraph{F1 score}

The F1 score is the harmonic mean between precision and recall.
\begin{equation}
    F1 =
    \frac{2 \cdot \text{Precision} \cdot \text{Recall}}
    {\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}
\end{equation}
It provides a balanced score when both precision and recall are important.


\paragraph{AP}

Average Precision measures detection accuracy across different confidence thresholds, computed as the area under the precision-recall curve.



\section{Tracking}

\paragraph{MOTA}

MOTA evaluates overall tracking accuracy by accounting for false positives, false negatives and identity switches.
\begin{equation}
    \text{MOTA} =
    1 - \frac{FN + FP + IDSW}
    {GT}
\end{equation}
where $FN$ denotes false negatives, $FP$ denotes false positives, $IDSW$ denotes the number of identity switches, and $GT$ denotes the number of ground truth objects. Higher values indicate better tracking performance.


\paragraph{IDF1}

IDF1 measures the accuracy of identity preservation during tracking.
\begin{equation}
    IDF1 = 
    \frac{2 \cdot IDTP}
    {2 \cdot IDTP + IDFP + IDFN}
\end{equation}
where $IDTP$, $IDFP$ and $IDFN$ represent identity true positives, false positives and false negatives respectively. Higher values indicate more consistent identity tracking.

\paragraph{ReLU}

ReLU is an activation function used for faster training and to reduce the vanishing gradient problem.
\begin{equation}
    \text{ReLU}(x) = 
    \begin{cases}
        0 & x \leq 0 \\
        x & x > 0
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}


\paragraph{Batch Normalisation}

Batch normalisation is a technique used to dramatically accelerate training of deep networks \cite{ioffe2015batchnormalizationacceleratingdeep}.
\begin{equation}
    y = \frac{x - \mathbb{E}[x]}{\sqrt{\text{Var}[x]+\epsilon}} * \gamma + \beta
\end{equation}
where $x$ is the input, $\mathbb{E}[x]$ and $\text{Var}[x]$ are the mean and variance computed per channel, $\epsilon$ is a small constant, and $\gamma$ and $\beta$ are learnable parameters.
